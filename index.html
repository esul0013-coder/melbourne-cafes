01

Where Are the Cafes?

The CBD is enormous, Carlton is legendary, and Docklands grew faster than anyone expected. Drag the year slider on the map and watch two decades of change play out across the city.

Cafe density across inner Melbourne

Colour shows cafe count per CLUE small area. Drag the year slider to explore how density shifted from 2002 to 2024. Melbourne (Remainder) covers the fringe parts of the 3000 postcode outside the CBD core, including Chinatown and the East End. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE 2002–2024.

Reading the map

The CBD is the obvious answer. Carlton is the more interesting one.

Six hundred and three cafes in 2.5 square kilometres. The CBD's numbers are hard to argue with, and the dark espresso colour on the map reflects that.

But Carlton, sitting directly to the north, tells a quieter and older story. The Italian migrant community that settled here in the 1950s built a coffee culture before the rest of Melbourne knew what a flat white was. That history shows up in the density numbers today.

Try dragging the slider back to 2002. Docklands barely registers. By 2012 it has grown into a serious cafe precinct, built almost entirely from scratch.

Tip: Hover any suburb to see exact cafe counts and total seats for that year.

Top suburbs by cafe count (2024)

The CBD has nearly four times more cafes than Carlton, which sits in second. The gap between first and second is larger than the gap between second and last. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE 2024.

The CBD leads by a mile, but Carlton earns its reputation differently

Carlton fits 164 cafes into 2.7 square kilometres. That kind of density, 60 cafes per square kilometre, is something very few places in Australia can match. It is the result of seven decades of coffee culture being layered into the same walkable streets.

The CBD's 603 cafes serve a different purpose entirely. They are there for the hundreds of thousands of workers and visitors who pass through every day, not for a neighbourhood community.

Cafe density — cafes per square kilometre by suburb (2024)

Raw cafe counts only tell part of the story. Density, meaning how many cafes fit into each square kilometre, reveals how walkable and concentrated each suburb's coffee scene actually is. Carlton at 60 cafes per square kilometre is second only to the CBD. Southbank, despite having some of the city's largest venues, sits at just 33 per square kilometre because its footprint along the river is relatively spread out. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE 2024.

02

Twenty Years of Growth

For almost two decades, Melbourne's cafe scene grew without stopping. Then 2020 happened, and the city recorded its first ever annual decline. What followed was equally remarkable.

Cafe count & annual growth rate, 2002–2024

The shaded area shows total cafe count per year on the left axis. The dashed orange line shows the year-on-year growth rate as a percentage on the right axis. Together they reveal not just how many cafes existed, but how quickly the scene was moving at any given moment. The 2020 dip is the only negative reading in 22 years of data. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE 2002–2024.

−5.7%

The first and only annual decline in the dataset. Melbourne's extended lockdowns closed venues permanently, many of which never reopened.

+3.8%

By 2024, the scene had not just recovered but returned to growth, reaching 1,073 cafes across the inner city.

Peak year

2018

Melbourne reached its pre-pandemic high of 1,126 cafes in 2018, a number it has not yet surpassed.

Cafe count by suburb and year

Each cell shows how many cafes existed in that suburb in that year. Darker means more cafes. The CBD's column stays dark throughout, but the more interesting story is Docklands, which goes from almost invisible in 2002 to a prominent presence by 2010 as the waterfront precinct was built from the ground up on former industrial land. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE 2002–2024.

How suburb rankings shifted, 2002–2024

Rank 1 means the most cafes in that year. The CBD has held first place without interruption. Carlton has held second for the entire 22-year period. The more interesting movement is in ranks three through six, where Docklands climbed steadily from outside the top six in 2002 to a consistent third by 2016, overtaking North Melbourne along the way. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE 2002–2024.

03

Inside vs. Outside

Where you sit matters as much as what you order. Melbourne's outdoor cafe culture is real, but it is far more concentrated than most people realise.

4.5:1

106,759 indoor seats versus 23,464 outdoor. Eight out of every ten dining seats in inner Melbourne are inside a building.

City-wide indoor vs outdoor (2024)

Source: City of Melbourne CLUE 2024.

Indoor vs outdoor seats by suburb (2024)

Southbank carries the highest outdoor proportion of any suburb, which makes sense given that its cafes and restaurants are built along a riverside promenade designed for open-air dining. The CBD leans heavily indoor, because its customers are office workers who need somewhere reliable regardless of what Melbourne's weather decides to do that morning. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE 2024.

Seating growth — Carlton, Docklands & East Melbourne

Each panel shows one suburb's indoor and outdoor seat count from 2002 to 2024, with its own y-axis scale so smaller suburbs remain readable. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE 2002–2024.

Seating growth — CBD, North Melbourne & Southbank

The CBD panel dwarfs every other suburb in this dataset. Southbank's outdoor seating grew sharply after 2010 as the riverside precinct expanded. North Melbourne's growth has been slow and steady throughout. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE 2002–2024.

What to look for

Where you sit tells you what kind of suburb you are in

Southbank and Docklands tilt outdoor because their cafes were designed around geography. A riverside promenade is not a space you enclose, it is a space you put tables in front of.

The CBD is the opposite. Its customers arrive on a schedule and need shelter from wind, rain, and the general unpredictability of Melbourne weather. Indoor seating is not a preference there, it is a requirement.

Carlton is somewhere in the middle, a neighbourhood that has both laneway terraces and warm interior rooms, because it serves both the lunchtime regular and the weekend lingerer.

If you want outdoor dining in Melbourne, Southbank is your best chance

No other inner suburb comes close to its outdoor seat count. On a good Melbourne day, there is genuinely nowhere better to sit outside with a coffee.

04

Hidden Gem Suburbs

Having the most cafes is not the same as having the best access. This section introduces a different way of measuring Melbourne's coffee scene, one that accounts for how many people are actually competing for those seats.

The gem score formula

Gem Score =
(Cafes per 1,000 residents
+ Seats per 1,000 residents) ÷ 2

Normalised to a 0 to 100 scale across 12 inner suburbs. A higher score means more cafe access relative to how many people live there. A score of 0 means the lowest ratio in the dataset, not zero cafes. All scores are relative within these 12 suburbs only.

The CBD has 603 cafes, but it also has the longest queues

Hundreds of thousands of workers, students, and tourists pass through the CBD every day. The number of cafes is impressive, but so is the number of people competing for them. Peak hour at a Melbourne CBD cafe is not a relaxed experience.

The gem score asks a more practical question. Where do cafes and seats genuinely outpace the number of people who live in the area? Those are the suburbs where walking in and finding a table is actually realistic.

Note that South Yarra's low score reflects a boundary issue in the CLUE dataset. Chapel Street's cafes fall largely outside the mapped area, so South Yarra looks underserved when it is not.

Cafes vs seats per 1,000 residents

Each bubble is a suburb. Suburbs in the top right corner have both high cafe density and high seat availability relative to their population. Those are the hidden gems. Melbourne CBD is excluded because it is an extreme outlier at 22 cafes and 2,704 seats per 1,000 residents. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE + ABS Census 2021.

Gem score ranking — all suburbs

All 12 suburbs ranked from 0 to 100. Docklands scores highest because its large waterfront venues provide an enormous number of seats relative to its modest residential population. A score of 0 means the lowest in this group, not an absence of cafes. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE + ABS Census 2021.

Where are Melbourne's hidden gem suburbs? (2024)

Darker colour means a higher gem score, which means more cafe access relative to resident population. Docklands, Carlton, and Southbank score well. The CBD sits in the mid-range despite its enormous supply, because the daily influx of workers and visitors makes competition for seats intense. Source: City of Melbourne CLUE 2024 + ABS Census 2021.

The verdict

Go to Docklands for your next coffee

It does not have the same reputation as Carlton or Southbank, but the numbers make a compelling case. Docklands has more dining seats per resident than anywhere else in the inner city. You will get a table, and you will not be waiting long for it.

Carlton for the coffee experience that built Melbourne's reputation

The highest cafe density per resident of any suburb, combined with a culture that has been developing since the 1950s. If you want the version of Melbourne coffee that people actually mean when they talk about Melbourne coffee, Carlton is where you go.
